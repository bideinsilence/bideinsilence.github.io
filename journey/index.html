My Journey

I'm a lifelong learner who's always been curious about computers and technology. I remember learning how to touch type by loading typing programs and simple games from 5 and 1/4 floppy disks on my mom's Tandy 1000 when I was growing up. At some point, we upgraded to an i486 with MS-DOS and Windows 3.x. On that machine, I discovered the rudimentary internet growing at the time by dialing in to local bulletin board services. I was immediately hooked after my first few times of keyboard menu exploring and simple web surfing. I felt then that the internet was the greatest invention of my lifetime.

In Highschool, I got the opportunity to study some simple robotics and low level programming. We learned about the electrical current flowing through transistor gates creating the offs and ons that become the 0's and 1's of binary code. We learned the boolean logic that allows those 0's and 1's to become machine code before an assembly language. My school's teaching concluded with an introduction to programming in BASIC and an overview of higher level programming fundamentals.

I went to college with no real direction about what I wanted to do in life other than an irrational fear of a desk job and the desire to get out of the small town where I grew up. So, I spent my time there fulfilling general credit requirements and focusing on science and mathematics classes that I’d always enjoyed. Unfortunately, I got so sick a few times in school (I would probably be dead if not for modern medicine) that I eventually ended up in a situation wherein I was paying off school loans while taking on new ones and also working near full time to afford also paying off my medical bills while continuing with school.

In my last semester, I decided that it was all too much stress and that I'd leave school to work full time at my current job and maybe return when I had a better idea of a career path or specific subject that I wanted to pursue. Many years of various odd jobs later, mostly bartending and working in kitchens, I had my first daughter. As my family started to grow, I decided I was ready for something new, a way to support my family without such long hours or late nights and with more consistent pay. I wanted a skill that might be able to help people with more than just a welcome ear, a good time out, or a nice meal. Since I'm just a big nerd who's always enjoyed learning, problem solving, do it yourself projects, and Linux, I decided to pursue programming given that I'd laid a foundation for this skill decades before.

I chose the Web as my path to explore software development because the internet is amazing (I’m often in awe that most of us walk around with a virtual Great Library of Alexandria in our pocket), and because my research told me that jobs in web development would continue to be in high demand. I first started with learning HTML and CSS, and while I still find proper implementations of each language impressive and interesting, their declarative nature didn’t immediately make me excited about learning to code. So, I decided to familiarize myself with more fundamental programming concepts like control flow, logic, and programmatic thinking at first a basic level in C and then further in Python and Javascript. I already needed to learn Javascript for the Web, and I liked that Python is taught at many different universities, can be used for general purposes, and that it has a strong emphasis on readability and conventions.

Not long after I chose to start learning about modern software engineering, I was fortunate to find a job working as an operations specialist for a major website based out of Seattle. My job didn’t directly involve any programming as I worked on a team that mostly attempted to stop fraud being committed through the site and help users comply with their user agreement. However, the site was built with the Python Django framework, so I got lots of experience traversing around the Django Admin. I also taught myself basic SQL so that I could run raw queries against the site’s massive database to be more effective in my investigations on fraud and site misuse.

While working there, my direct manager was very kind to arrange for me an opportunity to meet with a senior software developer and talk about his education and career and any advice he might have for someone wanting to learn enough about programming to work as a developer. He recommended that I stick with Python as it is great for learning and that I try to find some projects that I might be interested enough in to help stay motivated and create a more practical and intuitive course for learning. I ultimately left that job to be my children’s stay at home parent and use any free time I might find learning to code.

I continued on my path in web development with a focus on Django and Vue, although I did so after probably spending too much time learning how to use the Vim text editor and set it and my terminal environment to my preferences. Working in a terminal is fun and takes me back to the 80s, but it also taught me a lesson that I’ve since also heard from many different sources, “Read the Documentation.” So, that’s where I go first, MDN Web Docs for the Web and to the official source material for everything else. I prefer finding a tutorial, video, or course for help in further understanding a technology’s implementation or concepts only after I’ve visited it’s official documentation. And though I’ve chosen a particular tech stack to learn, I strive to remain language and tool agnostic. I intend to use the right tools for the right job and to remain curious about all of the many ways to approach and solve a problem. I look forward to continually learning as a never ending process.
